## What surprised me most?

Before this research, I expected the main debate to be a simple "AI will take jobs vs. AI won't take jobs" argument. What actually surprised me was how nuanced and technical the real discussions are. Engineers are not just debating employment — they are debating code quality, technical debt, learning paths, and what it even means to be a good developer. The depth of concern about junior developer pathways was especially unexpected. I had not considered that fewer entry-level tasks could mean fewer opportunities to learn through practice.

## What concerns me about AI in software engineering?

My biggest concern is the risk of engineers shipping code they do not understand. The research on trust in AI-generated code made me think carefully about a future where developers become more like reviewers of AI output than authors of their own solutions. If that review is done carelessly — which is a real risk when teams are under pressure to deliver fast — the result could be software that works today but fails in ways nobody can diagnose tomorrow.

I am also concerned about the junior developer pathway. As someone entering this field, it is uncomfortable to realize that the traditional route of starting with small, manageable tasks may be changing. If companies hire fewer juniors because AI handles beginner-level work, the path to becoming an experienced engineer becomes harder to find.

## What opportunities do I see?

I see a real opportunity for engineers who develop strong **critical thinking and review skills**. If AI generates code but humans must verify it, then the developer who can quickly spot a subtle bug, a security issue, or a poor architectural choice becomes extremely valuable. This is actually a higher-level skill than writing boilerplate code.

I also see opportunity in being someone who understands **both AI tools and software fundamentals deeply**. Engineers who know how large language models work, what they are good at, and where they fail will be better at using them than engineers who treat AI as a magic box.

## Which skills do I think will remain important?

Based on my research into the community discussions, I believe the following skills will remain critical:

- **Code review and critical reading of code** — more important than ever
- **System design and architecture thinking** — AI cannot understand a project's full context
- **Communication** — explaining decisions, requirements, and risks to teammates and clients
- **Debugging complex problems** — AI is weak at multi-layered, context-dependent bugs
- **Fundamentals** — understanding how memory, networks, and data structures work will help catch AI mistakes

## Did my opinion change after research?

Yes, somewhat. I came in thinking AI tools were mostly exciting and beneficial. I still think that, but I now have a much more balanced view. The community debates showed me that enthusiasm about AI productivity must be paired with discipline around code review, team conventions, and long-term maintainability. The engineers I found most credible in these discussions were not the ones dismissing AI or the ones blindly praising it — they were the ones thinking carefully about **when and how to use it responsibly**.

## How will I personally adapt?

My plan going forward is to:

1. **Learn AI tools actively** — use GitHub Copilot and similar tools during practice projects so I understand what they do well and where they fail
2. **Never skip understanding** — when AI generates code I use, I will make sure I can explain every part of it before submitting or deploying
3. **Build fundamentals strongly now** — before AI assistance becomes a crutch, I want to have a solid understanding of programming concepts so I can always evaluate AI output critically
4. **Follow community discussions** — Reddit, Hacker News, and engineering blogs are where real practitioners share honest experiences. Staying connected to those conversations will help me keep learning beyond formal courses.

The future of software engineering is uncertain in its details, but the direction is clear: engineers who think critically, communicate well, and use AI as a tool rather than a replacement for understanding will be the ones who thrive.
